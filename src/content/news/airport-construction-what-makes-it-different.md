Airport construction is not like any other commercial project. The complexity doesn't come from the construction itself, as the skills required are largely the same as any commercial build. The complexity comes from everything around the construction: the operating environment, the regulatory framework, the coordination requirements, and the zero-tolerance timeline expectations of airport operators.

Dunn Development has completed projects at both Ft. Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport (FLL) and Miami International Airport (MIA). Here's what we've learned about what makes these projects different, and what it takes to execute them successfully.

## The Operational Environment

The most fundamental difference in airport construction: the airport never stops operating. Every project phase must be planned around active passenger operations, ground traffic, aircraft movement areas, and security protocols.

This means:
- Work windows are often restricted to overnight or low-traffic periods
- Material staging and contractor access require coordination with airport operations teams days in advance
- Noise, dust, and vibration must be controlled to avoid impacting terminal operations
- Any work that could affect airfield line-of-sight or lighting requires FAA notification

On a typical commercial project, a scheduling conflict between two trades costs you a day. At an airport, a coordination failure can shut down a checkpoint or delay a gate. The stakes on every decision are higher.

## Security Requirements

Every worker on an airport construction project must clear a security background check and obtain the appropriate airport badge before setting foot on site. The badging process varies by airport authority and can take weeks.

For contractors, this means:
- Sub pre-qualification must happen well in advance of mobilization
- Worker lists must be finalized early and changes managed carefully
- Tools and materials entering secure areas go through inspection protocols

Experienced airport contractors build security lead times into their schedules from day one. Contractors who don't plan for this create their own delays.

## Multi-Agency Coordination

Airport projects typically involve coordination with multiple authorities simultaneously: the airport operator, the TSA, the FAA (for any airfield-adjacent work), the relevant municipality for permitting, and often the airlines whose operations are directly affected by the construction.

Getting alignment across all of these stakeholders, and keeping it as the project evolves, requires dedicated coordination that goes well beyond what a typical commercial project demands.

## What Owners and Tenants Should Know

If you're an airline, concession operator, or airport tenant planning a buildout or renovation, work with a contractor who has demonstrated airport experience, not just commercial experience.

Airport projects require a CM who:
- Understands badge and access protocols and builds them into the schedule
- Has existing relationships with airport operations and security teams
- Has experience writing Airport Construction Safety Plans (ACSPs) where required
- Can coordinate work windows with airline schedules and gate assignments

The construction phase of an airport project is often the smallest challenge. The coordination that surrounds it is where inexperienced teams fail.
